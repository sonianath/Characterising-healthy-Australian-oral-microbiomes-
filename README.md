The Concept of Oral Microbiome Transplantation
Oral Microbiome Transplantation (OMT) has emerged as a novel therapeutic intervention analogous to Faecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT), which has been successfully employed to treat Clostridioides difficile infections. The biological premise of OMT is the transfer of supragingival plaque or saliva from a healthy donor to a diseased recipient. By seeding the recipient's oral cavity with a diverse, stable, and commensal-rich community, OMT aims to modulate the dysbiotic environment, competitively exclude pathogens, and re-establish a biofilm associated with health. This approach aligns with the ecological plaque hypothesis, which posits that disease results from environmental shifts (such as low pH) that favor pathogenic proliferation; OMT seeks to reverse this by introducing a community capable of maintaining a neutral pH and resisting acidogenic shifts.

The "Super Donor" Phenomenon
A critical determinant of transplant success is the the donor oral microbiome. Evidence suggests the existence of "super donors" individuals possessing a uniquely diverse, stable, and pathogen-free microbiome capable of conferring therapeutic benefits upon transfer. Identifying these individuals is not merely a matter of finding those without active disease; it requires a granular characterization of the "healthy" microbiome and the factors that sustain it.
Recent investigations into the Australian population have begun to define this. Research indicates that the ideal donor profile is shaped significantly by sociodemographic and lifestyle factors. 
For instance, high microbial diversity—a marker of resilience—has been associated with specific demographics (e.g., non-Australian country of birth) and behaviors (e.g., infrequent recent dental visits, suggesting a natural resistance to disease without intensive intervention). Conversely, dietary habits, particularly the consumption of free sugars and fermentable carbohydrates, have been shown to drive the abundance of acidogenic taxa like Streptococcus, which mediate a reduction in salivary pH and compromise the protective nature of the biofilm.
